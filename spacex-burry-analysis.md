The Big Short Slams SpaceX Mania: Eager to Short Yet Options Too Costly, Not Even Worth $1 Trillion!
After SpaceX, Elon Musk’s conglomerate covering rockets, satellites and AI businesses, posted gains for three consecutive trading days following its Nasdaq listing, renowned short-seller investor Michael Burry publicly questioned the firm’s nearly $3 trillion market value on Tuesday, stating that he holds no shares of SpaceX stock whatsoever.
He added that he had intended to short SpaceX, yet the cost of put options proved excessively high, even unaffordable for the veteran investor famous for betting against the subprime mortgage market. Burry revealed that he had reviewed multiple put option transactions related to SpaceX but ultimately abandoned all plans.
Burry rose to fame for accurately shorting mortgage-backed securities ahead of the 2008 financial crisis, a legendary trade documented in Michael Lewis’ book The Big Short and its Oscar-winning film adaptation.
He returned to the public spotlight in late October last year after a long hiatus. Since then, he has repeatedly warned against stock market bubbles, disclosed new short positions on AI stocks including NVIDIA, and launched a paid Substack newsletter to share his independent investment insights freely.
“I have no positions related to SpaceX right now, neither short nor long,” Burry wrote in his Tuesday Substack post.
Specifically, Burry disclosed relevant option pricing data. When SpaceX stock traded at around $212, a put option with a strike price of $100 expiring in December 2028 cost roughly $25. A similar contract maturing in June 2027 was priced at approximately $13, while the December 2026 put option was valued at about $6.75.
He admitted he was tempted by the shortest-term contract but finally decided to pass. In his view, SPCX stock is likely to stabilize around the $200 range under favorable conditions, which will gradually ease volatility across option chains.
Nevertheless, Burry remained skeptical about its sky-high valuation. He described SpaceX essentially as a small-scale space enterprise, a niche telecom operator, a struggling social media firm and a miniature version of Coreweave, with annual revenue below $20 billion.
He pointed out that SpaceX’s current market cap has far surpassed that of many established corporations, personal fortunes and even the economic scale of numerous countries, taking Berkshire Hathaway led by Warren Buffett as a typical example.
“It outpaced Berkshire Hathaway by 2.5 times in just three days. Berkshire Hathaway was built through two centuries of relentless efforts by its founders, who are among the greatest investors of our era,” Burry commented.
His remarks further fueled debates over whether investors have overvalued SpaceX’s core businesses including launch services, satellite internet and social media ventures. SpaceX shares surged 20% on its listing day to hit record highs, with an overall rise of nearly 25% so far this week. This historic IPO has also made Elon Musk the world’s first trillionaire.
In fact, Burry had cast doubt on SpaceX’s valuation long before its official listing. He insisted that the company’s IPO documents failed to justify a $1 trillion valuation, let alone a $2 trillion one.
“All upward movements are merely driven by market hype and technical factors,” he stressed. “Nothing in its S-1 filing can prove it is worth one trillion dollars, not to mention two trillion.”
Last month, Burry advised investors to cut holdings in skyrocketing tech stocks and abandon excessive greed, warning that the frenzy surrounding artificial intelligence and momentum trading has inflated asset valuations. For months, he has cautioned that the market’s obsession with AI is increasingly resembling the final stage of the dot-com bubble.
